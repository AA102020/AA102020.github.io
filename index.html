Biography

I am a computational scientist and technology policy strategist working across institutional risk, banking, defense policy, conflict studies, AI governance, model risk management, consumer credit governance, regulatory technology, and the digital economy. My experience spans enterprise AI solutions, consumer-credit analytics, financial-data systems, treasury and risk data engineering, banking operations analytics, and advanced analytics in large-scale financial and public-sector environments.

My current work focuses on AI reliability evaluation, inspection regimes for AI-generated deliverables, consumer-finance intelligence, the formation of governance regimes, and algorithmic command in defense contexts. Across these areas, I study how computational systems, AI-enabled workflows, and data-driven infrastructures can be evaluated, documented, governed, and made reliable for institutional use.

My broader research interests include the use of machine learning and AI in defense policy, computational approaches to conflict studies, agent-based modeling for policy analysis, consumer credit governance, model risk management, and the interaction between regulatory frameworks and emerging technologies.

Research Agenda

AI, Defense Policy, and Conflict Studies

I study how machine learning and AI reshape defense policy, military decision systems, conflict analysis, command structures, and institutional accountability. This research includes algorithmic command, autonomous decision support, human oversight, operational opacity, and the policy dilemmas created when computational systems enter high-consequence security environments.

Agent-Based Modeling, Dynamic Networks, and Social Dynamics

I have studied agent-based modeling for water-resources management, agent-based modeling for policy analysis, dynamic networks, stochastic systems, and computational social dynamics. This work includes applications to water-resources systems, natural-systems modeling, cooperative networks, group behavior, and institutional decision problems.

I approach these topics as dynamic systems shaped by interacting actors, network structures, rules, incentives, feedback, uncertainty, spatial and environmental constraints, and institutional arrangements. The goal is to use computational modeling to make policy-relevant systems more interpretable, testable, and analytically tractable.

Formation of Governance Regimes

My governance research examines how rules, infrastructures, professional practices, classifications, review routines, evidentiary standards, and authority relations stabilize into governance regimes. I use this perspective to analyze consumer finance, AI governance, regulatory technology, and institutional review of technical systems.

Consumer Credit Governance and Model Risk

I study consumer credit governance as a sociotechnical field shaped by underwriting, pricing, servicing, disclosure, complaint handling, model risk management, regulatory compliance, fair-lending review, monitoring systems, and institutional accountability.

Regulatory Frameworks and Technology

I examine how regulatory frameworks adapt to computational systems, automated decisions, data infrastructures, AI-enabled analytics, and institutional risk. This includes RegTech, SupTech, supervisory data systems, AI assurance, auditability, documentation, and the governance of technical evidence.

Selected Works

Reliability of AI-Generated Analytic Deliverables

Reliability of AI-Generated Analytic Deliverables: A Stochastic Workflow Evaluation Procedure. A. Ahmed. Working manuscript, May 2026.

SSRN: https://ssrn.com/abstract=6789343

Develops a stochastic workflow evaluation procedure for AI-generated analytic deliverables, centered on repeated-run diagnostics, source-role traceability, workflow-choice discipline, task-conditioned comparison, reproducibility, reviewability, and use-specific decision thresholds.

Inspection Regimes and Institutional Standing

Decipimur Specie Recti: Inspection Regimes: How Generative AI Deliverables Become Valid and Admissible Work Products. A. Ahmed. Working paper, May 2026. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.20268016.

SSRN: https://ssrn.com/abstract=6776300

Develops an STS account of how AI-generated deliverables acquire institutional standing through registration, containment, inspection, classification, authorization, controlled circulation, maintenance, and reinspection.
